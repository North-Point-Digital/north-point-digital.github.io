If you've asked an agency "how much for an AI pilot?" and got "it depends", this
post is the answer they should have given you. Prices below are drawn from
published UK price guides and our own quoting, current as of mid-2026.

## The short answer

UK AI proof of concept pricing falls into three bands:

- **£5,000–£15,000 — feasibility checks.** Four to six weeks, one narrow
  question: can a model do this job with our data at all? Usually a freelancer
  or a small consultancy, often with throwaway code. You get an answer, not
  software.
- **£25,000–£85,000 — the standard single-use-case PoC.** Six to twelve weeks
  with a boutique or mid-tier consultancy. Working software built on your data,
  integration with one or two of your systems, and a report on what production
  would cost. This is the band most published UK guides quote.
- **£85,000+ — enterprise pilots.** Multiple use cases, heavy compliance,
  big-firm day rates. If you're a small business, you shouldn't be here.

Why the tenfold spread? Mostly the day rates underneath. UK freelance AI
practitioners charge roughly £400–£800 a day, boutique specialists £1,200–£2,500,
and the big firms £1,500–£3,000+. A six-week engagement is twenty to thirty
working days of senior time — multiply through and the bands above stop looking
arbitrary.

## What actually moves the price

Four things, in order of impact:

1. **The state of your data.** If what the AI needs lives in one system and
   exports cleanly, you're at the bottom of the band. If it's scattered across
   five tools and a shared drive, expect the difference to show up on the quote.
   Industry analyses consistently find data preparation adds 40–60% to AI
   budgets when it isn't scoped up front — make sure your quote includes it.
2. **How many systems the AI must touch.** A prototype that reads an export is
   cheap. One that writes back into your CRM, accounts package and inbox is not.
3. **The accuracy bar.** "Drafts a reply a human checks" is one price.
   "Sends the reply unsupervised" is a different project entirely — for a pilot,
   always start with the first.
4. **Who's doing the work.** See the day rates above. Worth knowing: a junior
   team at a low day rate frequently costs more overall, because the senior
   review you're not paying for shows up later as rework.

## The costs nobody puts on the quote

Ask any provider these three questions before signing:

- **What does it cost to run?** Model usage and infrastructure for a pilot are
  usually modest — tens to low hundreds of pounds a month — but production can
  be very different. If the proposal has no running-cost estimate, the thinking
  hasn't been done.
- **What happens to the prototype?** Some PoCs are built to be thrown away.
  That's legitimate, but you should know before you pay, not after.
- **Who owns the result?** Code, prompts and any fine-tuned models should be
  yours. If they're not, you're renting, and the price should reflect that.

## Fixed fee or day rate?

For a proof of concept, insist on a fixed fee. Roughly half of UK SME
engagements are now priced this way, and the logic is simple: a PoC is a
defined question with a defined end date, so an open-ended day rate transfers
all the risk to you. A consultancy that won't fix a price for its own
recommended scope is telling you something about its confidence.

## Grants can change the maths

Innovate UK runs AI proof of concept programmes where micro and small
businesses can claim back a large share of eligible costs — recent rounds
funded up to 70% on projects between £50,000 and £120,000. The thresholds suit
more ambitious pilots rather than first experiments, but if your use case is
substantial, it's worth checking eligibility before assuming the headline
price is what you'll pay.

## How to keep a pilot affordable

The cheap pilots aren't cheap because someone worked faster. They're cheap
because someone scoped harder:

- **One use case.** Not a "platform". The narrower the question, the less it
  costs to answer.
- **Sample data first.** Prove it on an export before paying for integrations.
- **Agree the kill criteria.** Decide up front what result means "stop". A PoC
  that can't fail is a brochure, and you shouldn't pay project rates for one.
- **Weekly demos.** If you can't see it taking shape, you're funding a surprise.

## What you should get for your money

Whatever you pay, the deliverables should look like this: working software
built with your own data, an honest running-cost projection for production, a
written route to live, and a recommendation — including "don't proceed" if
that's where the evidence points. A slide deck with a roadmap is not a proof
of concept, whatever the invoice says.
